Hyperpop or Bubblegum Bass is a loosely defined electronic music movement and microgenre that predominantly originated in the United Kingdom during the early 2010s. It is characterized by an exaggerated or maximalist take on popular music, typically integrating pop and avant-garde sensibilities while drawing on elements commonly found in electronic, hip-hop, and dance music. It is often linked to A. G. Cook, a musician from England, and his record label PC Music. Other prominent artists in the scene include SOPHIE, Charli xcx, and 100 gecs.

The hyperpop music scene comes from a mix of different music styles. Extreme pitching and warping of both the bass and rhythm are common, as well as upbeat, bubbly synths. Hyperpop often incorporates themes of leftist politics, gender expression, and meme culture. The genre stems mainly from Web 2.0 internet culture and Nightcore and consists of high levels of surrealism and genre-fluidity. Music associated with this scene received wider attention in August 2019 when Glenn MacDonald, an employee of Spotify, used the term "hyperpop" for the name of a playlist featuring artists such as Cook and 100 gecs.

The style gained popularity with younger audiences through social media, especially TikTok. Some people believe the COVID-19 lockdowns contributed to its spread. After the term "hyperpop" was used, many artists in this style expressed dislike for the label and even declared the microgenre "dead" starting in 2020.

History[]

Hyperpop originated in the 2010s as a new style of experimental pop music, coming from bubblegum bass. AG Cook’s record label, PC Music, played a big part in this by using creative music techniques like changing the pitch of voices, using strange beats, and lots of synthetic sounds. Artists assocated with PC Music, like AG Cook, Hannah Diamond, SOPHIE, Charli XCX, Danny L Harle, GFOTY, Namasenda, and Planet 1998, helped create hyperpop’s bold, futuristic sound. They mixed pop music with new and different sounds, and the genre grew quickly because artists could share their music on platforms like SoundCloud, reaching people all over the world.[1]

In 2019, Spotify listed an official hyperpop playlist, which further publicised the genre. This playlist consisted of 100 gecs, Slayyyter, Gupi, Caroline Polachek, Hannah Diamond, and Kim Petras.

The genre gained additional popularity on TikTok during the early 2020s, with a notable example including the song 'SugarCrash!'.[2][3]

Politics[]

Hyperpop is a leftist music subculture that challenges traditional gender roles and embraces progressive politics, much like the punk subculture. It defies mainstream trends in both sound and culture, celebrating gender fluidity, feminism, and queer liberation, with a particular focus on transgender and nonbinary experiences.[1] The microgenre’s emphasis on vocal modulation enables artists to experiment with the gender presentation of their voices and navigate gender dysphoria. Hyperpop artists, including Sophie and 8485, have explored themes of gender fluidity and selfhood in their lyrics.[4]

Visuals[]

Hyperpop visuals are a collage of various internet subcultures, heavily reminiscent of the Web 2.0 era, with elements like shiny UI components, pixelated graphics, and exaggerated branding aesthetics. The genre embraces the humor and irony of meme culture, creating chaotic, maximalist compositions. Due to the broadness of the term "hyperpop," there isn’t one definitive visual aesthetic. Instead, it blends influences from McBling/Baddie, Y2K Futurism, Glitchcore, and other styles, often creating a surreal, cyberculture-inspired visual experience.

Fashion[]

While there is no definitive "Hyperpop wardrobe" due to the genre-bending nature of subculture, there are common recurring motifs reminiscent of the Web 2.0 era and early internet culture. 2000s-inspired clothing, anime and furry motifs are common within the Hyperpop fandom. Hyperpop artists and fans have incorporated a wide range of seemingly contradictory aesthetics into the general fashion of the subculture, blending influences from mall goths, scene kids, Y2K Futurism, and McBling. The fashion style most commonly associated with Hyperpop is the 2020 alt kid, due to artists like 100 gecs gaining popularity on TikTok during the quarantine.

Music[]

PC Music[]

PC Music is a British record label founded in June 2013 by producer A. G. Cook. The label is known for its surreal or exaggerated take on pop music, creating more of an experimental and electronic pop sound. The label has inspired both praise and criticism from journalists, and has been called "polarizing".

Related Genres[]

Hyperpop encompasses a diverse range of subgenres and labels with overlapping characteristics and fluid boundaries. These classifications can be subjective, as artists often experiment with different sounds and influences. Some subgenres have distinguishing features, such as Dariacore, which emphasizes chaotic production. Others, like HexD vs. Sigilkore or Krushclub vs. Scenecore, share many similarities and can be difficult to differentiate, often relying on subtle distinctions in production techniques or aesthetic choices. The categorization of hyperpop music into these subgenres remains open to interpretation and artist preference.

Bubblegum Bass[]

Bubblegum Bass, often considered the first wave of hyperpop, is an electronic dance music genre that emerged in the early 2010s, primarily associated with the PC Music label. It is characterized by a novel approach to pop music, embracing authenticity and eclecticism while blurring the lines between high and low culture. Artists in this genre often re-appropriate pop music clichés and elevate them through unconventional sonic expressions. This is achieved through pitched-up vocals, off-kilter synths, and syncopated rhythms, creating a hyper-feminine and saccharine sound.

Bubblegum Bass draws influences from various genres, including UK Bass, Deconstructed Club, Electro House, Trap, and even 2000s-era Trance. This eclectic mix results in a diverse range of tracks, from chaotic and glitchy compositions to sentimental pop ballads and bombastic pop-rap. Visuals are a key component of bubblegum bass, often featuring hyperreal imagery, metallic objects, and LGBTQ+ themes. Artists explore concepts like feminism, identity, and post-internet anxiety through their music and visuals.

While the term "bubblegum bass" gained popularity in the late-2010s, the style was initially referred to as "PC music" due to its association with the PC Music label. As the genre gained traction, it influenced and crossed over with other genres, eventually leading to the emergence of hyperpop in the late 2010s and early 2020s.[5]

Dariacore[]

Dariacore, also known as Hyperflip, is an internet-based electronic dance music subgenre that emerged in the early 2020s. It is characterized by chaotic production, hard basslines, and the use of prominent vocal samples, often heavily edited and sped up. This subgenre draws influences from various genres, including Nightcore, mashup, and hardcore, and is known for its short, energetic songs with over-stimulating drops. It often references meme culture and the 2010s Internet era.

The term "Dariacore" was coined by Jane Remover under the alias leroy, and the genre gained popularity through online platforms like SoundCloud and Bandcamp. Artists like xaev, dashie, and gingus have contributed to the genre's development, often theming their releases around TV shows, video games, and other popular media. Dariacore has also seen crossover with digicore and hyperpop, with artists from these genres releasing their own interpretations.[6]

Digicore[]

Digicore is a form of hip-hop that developed in the late-2010s, characterized by synthesized, bombastic production and melodious, auto-tuned vocals, often with a distinctive vocal glitching effect. It draws influences from various electronic dance music genres, rock, and pop, resulting in a high-energy and eclectic sound.

Emerging from underground SoundCloud rap trends and inspired by artists like Drain Gang and 100 gecs, digicore was pioneered by artists like David Shawty, Yungster Jack, and members of the Bloodhounds collective. It gained further popularity through artists like d0llywood1, blackwinterwells, and midwxst, accompanied by a short-lived visual style known as "glitchcore," which emphasized the genre's glitchy vocals and DIY aesthetic. While it is closely related to hyperpop, digicore maintains a distinct identity rooted in hip-hop, with a heavier emphasis on trap-inspired production and vocal inflections. It often crosses over with pop-rap and other trap-related styles like Plugg, Trap Metal, and Drill.

Digicore is recognized for its DIY aesthetic, often featuring intentionally low-effort and oversaturated cover arts referencing video games and internet culture, particularly Roblox and Minecraft. The scene is dominated by large collectives like NOVAGANG, Bloodhounds, Helix Tears, and GoonnCity. Lyrical themes often revolve around personal and psychological subjects, including betrayal, self-doubt, and mental health.[7]

Glitchcore[]

Main article: Glitchcore

"Glitchcore" can refer to both a visual aesthetic and a microgenre of music related to Hyperpop and Digicore. The musical style is characterized by high-pitched vocals, sharp 808s, frequent hi-hats, and exaggerated use of distortion and glitch effects. Artists like 100 Gecs and Twikipedia are considered pioneers of Glitchcore music. However, whether it truly constitutes a distinct genre is a subject of debate.

One of the key tracks associated with Glitchcore is "NEVER MET!" by CMTEN featuring Glitch Gum. The track even features an official remix with 100 gecs, reinforcing its connection to the genre. Some argue that Glitchcore represents a younger cohort of hyperpop artists influenced by SoundCloud Emo Rap, with 100 Gecs and Drain Gang rapper Bladee as progenitors. A defining characteristic is the "glitch sound" – an unsteady repetition of syllables, also found in songs by artists like Wido and d0llywood1. However, the boundaries between Glitchcore and Hyperpop remain blurry, with some considering them nearly interchangeable.

Glitchcore is more easily defined as a visual aesthetic than a musical genre. It is characterized by visual chaos, with bright, cluttered colors, rapid transitions, and digital distortion effects. These visuals often accompany hyperpop and other high-energy genres. The aesthetic draws inspiration from various sources, including early internet aesthetics, video games, and anime. Glitchcore can be interpreted as the Gen Z take on the Glitch Art movement, which uses technological errors as a means of self-expression.[8]

HexD[]

Main article: HexD

HexD, emerging from the SoundCloud underground rap scene in the late-2010s, is characterized by heavily bit-crushed, often sped-up and pitched-up vocals, and highly compressed and distorted production. It typically utilizes existing material from genres like Trap, Cloud Rap, Emo Rap, Trance, Breakbeat, and other Electronic Dance Music styles. By incorporating sound effects such as delay and heavy reverberation, hexd aims to create a dreamy, murky, and surreal atmosphere, sometimes evoking nostalgia or a futuristic ambiance. The genre occasionally draws influence from Vaporwave and its associated hyper-real aesthetic, with some producers employing similar sample manipulation techniques.[9]

Originally developed by the Hexcastcrew group, HexD gained traction with Stacy Minajj's Rare RCB hexD.mp3 mix, which remixed Reptilian Club Boyz tracks. The genre's popularity surged in the following two years, leading to numerous DJ mixes primarily based on hip-hop and EDM. This distinct DJing style incorporated elements of Nightcore, Florida fast music, and chopped and screwed, with producers speeding up, cutting, and compressing existing tracks into a dense "hexd" soundscape.

A main derivative of hexd is "crushed trap," a term popularized by 999 Heartake Sabileye, who created numerous mixes featuring heavily bit-crushed trap songs. A related style, sometimes considered a competing term, is "surge," coined by Kittenpuke and popularized by Sticki and their label Dismiss Yourself. Dismiss Yourself's Surge Compilation Vol. 1 showcased many prominent HexD artists.

With a strong presence in the Rate Your Music community, hexd influenced other internet-based genres like Sigilkore and Digicore. Another notable derivative is "crushed trance" or Sextrance, produced by artists like Sienna Sleep, Visavril, and iANO. Visually, HexD often incorporates a low-quality, DIY aesthetic, drawing on video game and 2000s nostalgia, frequently using images and elements from anime.

Hyper Mandelão[]

Hyper Mandelão, also known as Hyperfunky, is a recent development in the Brazilian funk carioca genre, emerging in the late-2010s from experimentations with funk beats and electronic music elements. It is characterized by a distinct electronic and futuristic sound, pushing the boundaries of traditional funk carioca and incorporating influences from hardcore electronic music. Notable artists include DJ Mu540, DJ Ramemes, and Pabllo Vittar.

This subgenre is heavily reliant on technology, utilizing digital audio workstations and electronic instruments to create its unique sound. Producers often use a combination of computer sound mixing programs, MIDI controllers, and even turntables to craft their beats. Hyper Mandelão distinguishes itself from other funk carioca subgenres through its more pronounced integration of electronic music elements and its emphasis on creating a highly artificial and electronic sound.

One of the defining characteristics of Hyper Mandelão is the use of a distinct sound called "tuim" (or "tuin"), which is often described as a continuous whistle-like sound that creates a sense of heightened energy and excitement.[10]

Krushclub[]

Main article: Krushclub

Krushclub is a microgenre that combines elements of EDM, HexD, and Jersey Club, drawing inspiration from house, techno, and electro-pop. It is characterized by its energetic rhythm, often featuring a Jersey Club-based or four-on-the-floor beat, bit-crushed vocals, and lo-fi production. Krushclub also frequently incorporates samples from and references to anime and video games.

Krushclub emerged from the online underground music scene, gaining popularity among Gen Z TikTok users in 2023. Lumi Athena is considered a primary pioneer of the genre, with their 2022 single "Smoke It Off!" achieving mainstream prominence through streaming services and TikTok. Other notable Krushclub artists include Odetari, cade clair, 9lives, and xxanteria.

The genre's visuals often feature pixelated imagery, stars, hearts, butterflies, and monochrome colors, reminiscent of Y2K Futurism and old web aesthetics. It also incorporates elements like low-poly models and scanline filters.

Scenecore[]

For the visual counterpart, see the Scenecore section on 2020 TikTok.

Scenecore is a subgenre of hyperpop that draws inspiration from the Scene subculture and Nightcore. It is characterized by pitched-up vocals, "nostalgic" melodies, and samples from video games and anime. Lyrical themes vary from artist to artist but often explore edgier topics compared to the music of the original Scene subculture, like mental health, gore, drugs, and the unknown. Scenecore emerged in the early 2020s, gaining popularity on social media platforms such as TikTok and SoundCloud.

Artists like h3artcrush, Rebzyyx, 15forevr, Kid Trash, and Xaxanity influenced the genre's early sound. h3artcrush's 2020 single "On the Floor" is considered a key track in the development of Scenecore, with its combination of glitchy synths, distorted vocals, and dark lyrical themes. Other notable Scenecore tracks include 15forevr and h3artcrush's "Sacrifice," Xaxanity's "BLOOD! BLOOD! BLOOD!," and Kid Trash's "I'M SORRY I LIKE EATING PEOPLE!!!!!!!!!!"[11] These tracks often feature themes of mental anguish, drug use, and violence.

Scenecore is evolving and has seen shifts in its style and influences. In 2023, many Scenecore artists began sampling 2000s Pop and Eurodance music. Genres like Krushclub and Sigilkore frequently overlap with Scenecore. In its early days, the genre used to be frequently depicted along an aesthetic inspired by 2020 Alt and Glitchcore. However, that aesthetic has fallen out of favor for other aesthetics that better reflect the themes of the songs, like Club (e.g. "party addict" by kets4eki) or Gorecore.

A segment of Scenecore songs contain themes of gore, self-harm, and even sexual abuse, which has attracted criticism for potentially romanticizing these topics. There are also many controversies surrounding specific artists, with allegations including child grooming (specifically against Punkinloveee), pedophilia, hate speech, exploitation of minors, racism, neo-nazism, scamming, and other actions that cause emotional and psychological harm. Such allegations of racism have led to accusations of these artists being "posers" within the scene, as it contradicts the inclusive values associated with the original Scene and Rave subcultures.

Sigilkore[]

Main article: Sigilkore

Sigilkore, evolving alongside HexD within the SoundCloud underground scene, is an experimental form of trap characterized by its dark, brooding lo-fi atmosphere, disorienting and busy production, pitched-up vocals, and layers of digital audio workstation effects. The name "Sigilkore" reflects the genre's visual aesthetic, which often incorporates dark magic, demonic symbology, deep web imagery, and other occult elements. Musically and visually, Sigilkore descends from "dark trap" and shares characteristics with other trap subgenres like Tread and Plugg, as well as Digicore and HexD due to its hyperactive, digitized approach.

The origins of Sigilkore can be traced back to affiliates and related artists of SpaceGhostPurrp's BMB Deathrow, particularly Jewelxxet and its key members like Luci4 and Sellasouls. Early releases such as Ashview Heights Legend and other works from these collectives laid the groundwork for Sigilkore's development. The song "Bodypartz" gained significant online virality, pushing sigilkore beyond its underground origins.

Visually, Sigilkore employs a maximalist use of sigils and references to occult worshipping, video games, anime, and elements from both recent and 2000s subcultures. It draws inspiration from genres like Cloud Rap, HexD, Tread, and Hyperpop, blending Trap drums with a more ambient approach, faster tempos, and experimental mixing and mastering techniques. These techniques include bass-boosted 808s, wide reverb-soaked synths, and a bitcrush effect inspired by HexD. Sigilkore also features stuttering beat and vocal cuts, alongside creatively manipulated sections that seamlessly transition between slowed-down and sped-up moments.

Lyrically, Sigilkore utilizes common trap themes but with an edgier flair, embracing dark imagery with references to vampires, blood, spellcasting, and demonic invocations. The genre originated from the music collective Jewelxxet, with members axxturel/luci4, 4jay, and islurwhenitalk credited with its creation.
